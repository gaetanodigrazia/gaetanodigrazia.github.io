# Intro
At the basis of artificial intelligence there are the concepts of intelligence and calculation which are, for them, concepts that are sometimes ambiguous and borrowed from the everyday life of human beings.
In this specific case, however, we are looking at the world of machines that are capable of processing information.

The brain processes information in parallel, while conventional computers do so in series; we will therefore have to build new types of parallel computers to make progress in AI.

Among other things, the conventional computer is based on true-false (binary) logic while truly intelligent systems will have to use a certain type of logic called "*fuzzy*"; to what was said above we must add the difficulty in recreating neurons as complex as animal ones: not fast (because their speed order is millisecond) but *complex*.

## Turing Test
In 1950, Turing did a test in which he involved 3 people: A a man, B a woman and C who is an interviewer (the latter in a separate room from the other two and connected with them via teleprinter).

The objective of the game for the interviewer was to determine which of the other two (A, B) which for him are labeled with (X, Y) is a man and which is a woman through the formulation of questions, such as: "Do I would you please say X the length of your hair?"; A's goal in the game is to try to make C misidentify while B's goal is to help C.

The next step of the Turing test is to replace A with a **machine** and ensure that it manages to deceive the interviewer by not being discovered: now, therefore, the text becomes machine-man-interviewer.
